## Preface

For many readers, Leviticus is the place where good intentions to read through the Bible quietly come to an end.

Its pages are filled with sacrifices, priests, ritual laws, festivals, and regulations that seem far removed from modern life. We encounter instructions about offerings, clean and unclean animals, skin diseases, holy days, and the architecture of worship. It can feel like a book belonging to another world—a world of tents, altars, and smoke that no longer has anything to say to us.

Yet what if we have been asking the wrong question?

Instead of asking, *"How do these regulations apply to my life?"* perhaps we might ask, *"What do these symbols reveal about the heart of God?"*

That question changes everything.

Leviticus stands at the center of the Torah. Genesis tells the story of humanity's creation and the loss of communion in the garden. Exodus tells of God's deliverance of Israel from slavery and His decision to dwell among His people. Leviticus answers the question that naturally follows: **How does a holy God live in the midst of a people who are still learning to trust Him?**

The answer is not found merely in laws or rituals. It is found in God's astonishing desire to remain near.

Again and again, beneath every sacrifice, every feast, every priestly blessing, every Sabbath, and every act of cleansing, one quiet promise continues to emerge:

*"I will set my tent among you... I will walk among you, and will be your God, and you will be my people."*

That promise became the lens through which these devotionals were written.

This book does not attempt to be a verse-by-verse commentary on Leviticus, nor does it seek to answer every historical or theological question surrounding Israel's worship. Many excellent books have already done that work. My hope has been something simpler: to read Leviticus contemplatively—to look through its symbols rather than stopping at them, allowing them to become windows into the character of God.

As I have read these ancient pages, I have become increasingly convinced that God has always met humanity where we are.

Israel had emerged from generations of slavery into a world where worship was often marked by fear, distance, and uncertainty. Rather than overwhelming His people, God patiently formed them. Through rhythms of worship, sacred time, holy space, and visible symbols, He taught them what His heart had always desired. Like a wise teacher leading children one careful step at a time, He invited them to draw nearer than they had ever imagined possible.

The sacrifices themselves seem to participate in that patient journey. Scripture eventually tells us that the blood of bulls and goats could never, by itself, remove sin. The prophets remind us that God delights in mercy more than sacrifice. And in Christ, we discover the fulfillment toward which every symbol has been pointing—not the abolishing of God's desire, but its perfect revelation. The God who once dwelt behind a veil now walks among us. The One who filled the tabernacle with His glory now makes His home within His people through the Holy Spirit.

The movement of Scripture is remarkably beautiful.

It begins in a garden where God walks with humanity.

It continues through the tabernacle in the wilderness and the temple in Jerusalem.

It reaches its fullness in Jesus Christ, the Word made flesh who "tabernacled" among us.

It continues through the Church, where ordinary men and women become living temples of God's presence.

And it culminates in the breathtaking promise of Revelation: *"Behold, God's dwelling is with humanity."*

From beginning to end, the story is the same.

God is drawing near.

If there is one hope I have for this book, it is that you will come to see Leviticus differently. Not as a collection of obsolete regulations, but as another chapter in the long story of God's faithful pursuit of humanity. A story in which every altar, every lamp, every loaf of bread, every festival, every priestly garment, and every sacrifice quietly whispers of a God whose deepest desire has always been communion.

As you read these reflections, don't worry about mastering every detail. Read slowly. Sit with the images. Allow the symbols to become companions in prayer. Let the tabernacle remind you that God longs to dwell with you. Let the Sabbath teach you to rest. Let the sacrifices point you toward the self-giving love of Christ. Let the wilderness become a place where trust can quietly grow.

Above all, remember that this journey does not end in a temple made with human hands.

It ends with God walking once again among His people.

May these pages help you hear His footsteps.

May they awaken within you a deeper trust in His goodness.

And may they encourage you to draw near to the One who has been drawing near to you all along.
