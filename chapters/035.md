## Day 35 — Dwelling in Booths

**Leviticus 23:33–43**

**John 1:14**

> *"Sometimes the holiest home is the one that reminds us God has always been our true dwelling place."*

Each year, after the harvest had been gathered and the fields had yielded their abundance, Israel stepped out of the comfort of permanent homes and lived for a time in simple shelters made from branches. It was a joyful feast, yet its joy was rooted in remembrance. These fragile booths recalled the wilderness journey, when God's people owned little, carried only what they could bear, and discovered that His presence was more dependable than walls of stone. Their security had never rested in sturdy houses but in the God who journeyed with them through every changing landscape.

There is something wonderfully liberating about this practice. We naturally seek permanence in things that cannot remain. We build our identity upon careers, possessions, accomplishments, reputations, or carefully arranged plans, hoping they will provide lasting peace. Yet life has a way of reminding us that much of what seems permanent is surprisingly fragile. Seasons change. Health falters. Relationships evolve. Dreams are fulfilled, and sometimes they are not. The Feast of Booths gently loosens our attachment to what cannot ultimately hold us, inviting us instead to discover the One whose faithfulness remains through every season of change.

The booths themselves were humble. They let in the wind, filtered the sunlight through leaves, and allowed the stars to be seen at night. They were never meant to hide people from the world but to help them remember their dependence upon God. Perhaps our own moments of vulnerability serve a similar purpose. The places where we feel exposed, uncertain, or unfinished may become the very places where we notice God's nearness most clearly. When our illusions of self-sufficiency begin to fall away, we often discover that we have been upheld by grace all along.

John echoes this ancient feast with breathtaking simplicity: *"The Word became flesh and lived among us."* The language he chooses carries the image of pitching a tent, of God coming to dwell in the midst of His people once again. In Jesus, the divine presence no longer resides behind the curtains of a tabernacle or within the walls of a temple. God enters the ordinary conditions of human life. He knows dusty roads, shared meals, tears, laughter, weariness, friendship, and loss. The One who once journeyed with Israel through the wilderness now walks beside humanity in the flesh, revealing that God's deepest desire has always been to make His home with us.

This changes how we understand our own pilgrimage. We are not wandering through life trying to find God as though He were hidden somewhere beyond the horizon. Rather, we are learning to recognize the One who has already chosen to accompany us. Every kitchen table can become a sanctuary. Every quiet walk can become holy ground. Every conversation marked by compassion can become another place where Christ dwells. We need not wait until life feels settled before experiencing His presence. Often He meets us most tenderly in the temporary places, where our hearts are open enough to receive Him.

From the garden where humanity walked with God, to the wilderness booths that remembered His faithful care, to the Word who pitched His tent among us, and finally to the New Creation where *"the dwelling of God is with humanity,"* Scripture tells one continuous story of divine companionship. God has never sought merely to visit His people. He has always desired to dwell with them. As we loosen our grip on temporary shelters and learn to make our home in His love, we discover that wherever Christ is present, we are already closer to home than we ever imagined.

> *Today I will rest in the presence of the God who has chosen to make His home with me.*
