## Day 17 — Seeing with Compassion

**Leviticus 13:1–8**

**Mark 1:40–45**

> *"The eyes of love do not first ask, 'What is wrong with you?' but, 'How may I draw near without losing compassion?'"*

As Leviticus turns to matters of disease and cleansing, it invites us into one of the most tender and difficult realities of human life: our vulnerability. A suspicious mark upon the skin required careful attention, not hurried judgment. The priest did not simply glance and condemn. He looked, waited, examined again, and only then made a decision. Even in these ancient instructions, there is a quiet refusal to let fear have the final word. Patience made room for truth, and careful discernment became an act of care for both the individual and the community.

There is wisdom here that reaches far beyond physical illness. How quickly we can label ourselves or others after seeing only a small part of the story. A painful conversation, a visible failure, an old wound, or a single mistake can become the lens through which we see an entire person. Yet God teaches His people to look more carefully. Love does not rush to conclusions. It is willing to pause, to observe with humility, and to remember that every human being bears a story that cannot be understood at first glance. Compassion begins with learning to see patiently.

When the leper approaches Jesus in Mark's Gospel, he carries more than a disease. He bears the weight of isolation, rejection, and the quiet ache of being regarded as untouchable. Before Jesus heals him, He does something astonishing: He reaches out and touches him. Compassion precedes correction. Relationship comes before restoration. The touch itself announces what the man's heart most longs to hear: *You are not abandoned. You are not beyond the reach of God's love.*

This is the glory of Christ. He fulfills the deepest purpose hidden within Leviticus, not by ignoring human brokenness but by entering into it without fear. Where others stepped back, He stepped forward. Where others saw impurity, He saw a beloved child longing to come home. His holiness was never fragile, as though it might be diminished by contact with suffering. Instead, His holiness was radiant enough to overcome what was broken with the healing power of divine love. In Jesus, holiness moves toward pain rather than away from it.

Perhaps there are places within us that still feel untouchable. Old shame, hidden grief, lingering regret, or wounds we have carefully concealed may seem like marks that separate us from God or from one another. We may even carry silent judgments against ourselves that are harsher than anything another person has ever spoken. Yet Christ approaches these places with the same compassionate hand He extended to the leper. He is never repelled by our weakness. He draws near, not to shame us, but to restore us to communion. The journey of healing often begins the moment we allow ourselves to believe that His love is greater than our fear.

The same compassion He offers us becomes the compassion He forms within us. We begin to look at others more slowly, listening before assuming, blessing before criticizing, and remembering that every face we encounter carries hidden joys and hidden burdens. The Church becomes most like Christ when it is known not for how quickly it categorizes people, but for how faithfully it bears one another's wounds. This is how the kingdom quietly grows: one gentle touch, one patient conversation, one merciful gaze at a time. The God who dwelt in the tabernacle now dwells among His people, teaching us to see one another with the compassionate eyes of Christ.

> *Today I receive Christ's compassionate touch and offer that same compassion to everyone I meet.*
