## Day 14 — Holy Fire

**Leviticus 10:1–3**

**Hebrews 12:29**

> *"The fire of God does not consume our humanity; it consumes whatever keeps us from love."*

Few passages in Leviticus are as sobering as the story of Nadab and Abihu. We are not told everything that lay within their hearts, nor are we invited to speculate beyond what the text reveals. Instead, we are left standing in silence with Aaron, who says nothing at all. It is a silence that reminds us there are moments when mystery is more faithful than certainty. Yet even within this difficult passage, the invitation is not to live in fear of God, but to recognize that drawing near to the Holy One is never a casual thing. Love is freely given, but it is never trivial.

Throughout Leviticus, fire is the sign of God's own presence. It warms the altar, receives the offerings, and bears witness that God dwells among His people. Fire gives light, purifies precious metals, bakes bread, and gathers families around its warmth. It is a gift, but it is also a reality that cannot be manipulated or possessed. We do not command fire; we learn to live wisely in its presence. In much the same way, God's holiness is not something we control or shape according to our preferences. It is the beautiful reality of God being wholly God—perfect in love, truth, mercy, and life.

When Hebrews declares, "Our God is a consuming fire," it echoes this ancient image while placing it in the light of Christ. The God revealed in Jesus is not divided against Himself. The fire that burns in the bush without destroying it, the fire that rests upon the disciples at Pentecost, and the fire that refines the heart all belong to the same holy love. God's holiness is not opposed to His mercy. It is His mercy in its purest form. Wherever His presence enters, everything false, destructive, and lifeless begins to give way so that what is true may flourish.

This is why the spiritual life often feels like both comfort and refining. We ask God to come near, and He gently exposes the resentment we have carried for years. We pray for peace, and He invites us to release the control we have mistaken for security. We seek His presence, and He uncovers the fears that have quietly ruled our decisions. His fire is not directed against us, but against all that diminishes the life He longs to share with us. The refining may be uncomfortable, yet it is always in service of restoration.

Perhaps "strange fire" can also become a question we quietly carry into our own hearts. What fuels my life? Is it the fire of ambition, fear, resentment, or the constant need to prove myself? Or is it the quiet flame of God's own Spirit—patient, gentle, steadfast, and free? We need not answer with shame. The God who invites us near also provides the very fire that He asks us to tend. We are not called to manufacture holiness, but to receive it, allowing His love to become the deepest source of our thoughts, our actions, and our desires.

Aaron's silence is a fitting place for today's meditation to end. Not every mystery is meant to be solved, but every mystery can become an invitation to deeper trust. We stand before a God whose holiness is beyond our grasp and whose mercy is beyond our deserving. His fire is not the fire of rejection but of transformation. As we draw near through Christ, we need not fear being consumed ourselves. Instead, we may quietly welcome the burning away of all that keeps us from becoming fully alive in the love that has always sought to dwell within us.

> *Today I welcome the holy fire of God's love, trusting it to refine my heart with mercy and grace.*
