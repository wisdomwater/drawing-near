## Day 10 — The Fire That Never Goes Out

**Leviticus 6:12–13**

**Hebrews 12:28–29**

> *"The fire of God's presence is not kept alive by fear, but by the faithful love that returns to Him each day."*

At the heart of the tabernacle, the fire upon the altar was never to go out. Morning after morning, before the camp stirred to life, the priest would place fresh wood upon the embers. The flame was tended with quiet faithfulness, not dramatic effort. It became a visible reminder that God's invitation to communion did not belong to isolated moments of spiritual excitement but to the steady rhythm of everyday life. The fire endured because it was lovingly attended.

There is something deeply reassuring about this image. God does not ask His people to create the fire, only to care for it. The flame itself is His gift; their calling is simply to make room for it. So much of the spiritual life is like this. We cannot manufacture love, peace, or holiness by sheer determination. But we can return, again and again, to the practices that keep our hearts open to the One whose life already burns within us.

The writer of Hebrews calls God "a consuming fire." These words have often been heard with trembling, as though God's fire exists primarily to threaten or destroy. Yet throughout Scripture, God's fire more often reveals His presence than His anger. It burns in the bush without consuming it. It rests upon the tabernacle as a pillar of fire by night. At Pentecost, it appears as tongues of flame resting gently upon ordinary men and women. Divine fire is the warmth of God's holy love, illuminating what is true, refining what is harmful, and awakening what has long lain dormant.

This is the fire that Christ came to kindle in human hearts. It is not the feverish heat of religious striving, but the quiet flame of love that slowly transforms us from within. It burns in forgiveness offered when resentment would be easier. It glows in patient endurance through seasons of uncertainty. It flickers in whispered prayers, hidden acts of kindness, and the simple decision to begin again after yesterday's failures. The kingdom often advances not through great bursts of spiritual intensity, but through countless small moments of faithful presence.

Perhaps tending the fire today looks very ordinary. It may be opening the Scriptures before the day's demands crowd in. It may be sitting in silence for a few moments, allowing your breathing to become a prayer. It may be offering gratitude before a meal, extending compassion to someone who tests your patience, or noticing the beauty of the morning sky. These practices do not earn God's nearness. They simply add fresh wood to the altar, making space for the flame that has never ceased to burn.

The promise hidden within Leviticus is that God's desire to dwell among His people is not a passing enthusiasm. His love is steadfast. His presence is enduring. Even when we feel only cold ashes, He has not abandoned the altar. The embers of grace remain, waiting for the gentle breath of our attention. Day by day, as we return to Him, we discover that the truest fire in our lives is not our devotion to God, but God's unwavering devotion to us. His love continues to burn, inviting us into a kingdom that cannot be shaken.

> *Today I quietly tend the flame of God's abiding presence within me.*
