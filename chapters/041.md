## Day 41 — Do Not Fear

**Leviticus 25:18–22**

**Matthew 6:25–34**

> *"Trust begins where our need to control finally comes to rest in the care of God."*

The Sabbath year asked Israel to do something that seemed impossible. They were to leave the fields unsown, allowing the land to rest while trusting that God would provide enough from the previous harvest to carry them through. It is easy to imagine the questions rising in anxious hearts: *What will we eat? What if there is not enough?* God did not dismiss those fears or shame His people for asking them. Instead, He answered them with a promise. The blessing would come before the need became desperate. The harvest of the sixth year would quietly become the provision for the seventh, reminding them that their lives were sustained not merely by their labor, but by the faithful generosity of the One who gave the harvest in the first place.

There is something deeply healing in this invitation. We often imagine that everything depends upon our own effort. We measure tomorrow by today's strength, convinced that if we stop striving for even a moment, everything will begin to unravel. Yet the Sabbath year gently loosens our grip. The resting fields become a living parable, teaching that creation itself is upheld by God's continual care. Even the soil is allowed to breathe. Even the earth is invited to trust its Creator. The God who formed the garden has never ceased tending it.

Jesus echoes this same melody on the hillside as He points toward birds in the sky and lilies scattered across the fields. He asks His listeners to pay attention to the ordinary miracles surrounding them. The birds do not awaken each morning consumed by fear, and the flowers do not anxiously compete for beauty. Their lives are received as gifts. Jesus is not inviting us into carelessness, nor is He suggesting that work has no place. Rather, He is inviting us to discover that beneath all our work is a deeper reality: our heavenly Father already knows what we need. Before we ask, before we strive, before we worry, we are already held within His attentive love.

Most of us know the quiet ache of wondering whether there will be enough. Enough time. Enough money. Enough health. Enough wisdom for the decisions before us. Anxiety has a way of pulling tomorrow into today until the soul begins carrying burdens it was never meant to bear. It scatters our attention, making it difficult to notice the grace that is already present. The invitation of both Leviticus and Jesus is not to pretend uncertainty does not exist, but to meet uncertainty with a deeper confidence. God's provision often arrives one day at a time, like manna in the wilderness, teaching us to live by trust instead of fear.

Seeking first the Kingdom is not another task to accomplish before God will care for us. It is a reorientation of the heart. It is learning to place our attention where life truly begins—with the presence of God already dwelling among us. As we seek Him, we begin to discover that peace is not found in securing every possible outcome, but in walking with the One who remains faithful through every season. The future is no longer an enemy to conquer, because it belongs to the same God who has carried us this far.

From the fields that rested in Israel, to the birds that glide through the open sky, to Christ who calls us away from anxious striving and into the Father's care, Scripture tells one continuous story of divine faithfulness. The God who dwelt with His people in the wilderness is the same God who dwells with us now, quietly providing daily bread, unseen mercies, and strength for each new morning. We need not possess tomorrow before it arrives. It is enough to receive this day as a gift, trusting that the hands which hold today are already preparing the grace we will need for tomorrow.

> *Today I will rest in the faithful care of the God who already knows what I need.*
