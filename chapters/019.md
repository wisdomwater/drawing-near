## Day 19 — The God Who Dwells

**Leviticus 15:31**

**Revelation 21:3**

> *"From the beginning, God's deepest desire has been not simply to be worshiped, but to dwell with His people."*

Hidden within a single verse near the end of Leviticus 15 is one of the great themes of the entire book. The concern is not merely about uncleanness or ritual. The reason given is far more beautiful: *"my tabernacle that is among them."* God had chosen to live in the midst of His people. Every instruction surrounding the tabernacle existed to protect and nurture that sacred communion. The heart of Leviticus has never been the keeping of rules for their own sake. It has always been about making room for the Holy One who delights to dwell among humanity.

This has been God's longing from the very beginning. In the garden, He walked with Adam and Eve in the cool of the day. After the wilderness came the tabernacle, where His glory rested among Israel. Later came the temple, a sign that heaven had drawn near to earth. None of these places, however, were the final destination. They were signposts pointing toward an even greater promise. God's purpose was never simply to inhabit sacred buildings, but to restore a people who could live in joyful communion with Him.

That promise takes flesh in Jesus. John's Gospel tells us that the Word became flesh and "dwelt among us," pitching His tent in the midst of our world. In Christ, God no longer remains behind curtains or within holy places made by human hands. He walks dusty roads, sits at ordinary tables, embraces children, heals the sick, and welcomes sinners. The dwelling place of God becomes a human life filled with grace and truth. Through Him, the distance between heaven and earth begins to disappear.

The Spirit continues this beautiful movement. God's presence is no longer confined to one mountain, one nation, or one sanctuary. Every heart opened to Christ becomes a place where His life takes root. Every community shaped by love becomes another living temple. We often imagine that we must journey far to find God, yet the gospel gently reverses the story. Again and again, it is God who journeys toward us. The One who walked in Eden, filled the tabernacle, overshadowed Mary, and raised Christ from the dead is still making His home wherever love is welcomed.

The final pages of Scripture reveal where this long pilgrimage has always been leading. John hears the triumphant announcement: *"Behold, God's dwelling is with people."* The Bible does not end with souls escaping the earth to find God somewhere else. It ends with God coming to dwell fully among His creation, wiping away every tear and making all things new. The garden returns, not as a memory but as a renewed creation where nothing remains to separate God from His beloved children. The thread that began in Genesis finds its joyful completion in Revelation.

Until that day, we live as people of the tabernacle. We carry within us the quiet assurance that God has already drawn near and continues to do so each day. Every act of forgiveness prepares a room for His presence. Every shared meal becomes a reminder of His table. Every prayer whispered in the stillness, every kindness offered without recognition, every burden quietly carried with another becomes another place where heaven touches earth. The God who dwells among His people has never abandoned His purpose. His home has always been with us, and through Christ He is gently preparing our hearts—and ultimately all creation—to become a fitting dwelling place for His everlasting love.

> *Today I welcome the God who has always desired to make His home with us.*
