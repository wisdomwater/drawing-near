## Day 3 — An Open Hand

**Leviticus 1:3–9**

**Romans 12:1–2**

> *"Love does not ask first what we possess, but whether our hands are open."*

The burnt offering is the first sacrifice described in detail, and it is marked by one striking feature: nothing is held back. The offering is placed upon the altar until all of it is given over to the fire. To modern ears, the imagery can feel unfamiliar, even unsettling. Yet beneath the ritual lies a deeply human question: What does it look like to entrust ourselves completely to the God who has drawn near? The fire is not merely about loss. It is about surrender that makes room for communion.

Before the flames are kindled, the offering is carefully prepared. It is washed with water, arranged with care, and placed upon the wood piece by piece. Nothing is hurried or careless. There is a quiet reverence in every movement, as though even the smallest act of offering deserves loving attention. The path into God's presence is not rushed. Love lingers over what it gives.

When Paul writes to the Romans, the altar appears again, but something has changed. No longer are we invited to place an animal upon the fire. Instead, we are invited to present ourselves as "a living sacrifice." This is not a call to grim self-denial or relentless striving. It is an invitation to become people whose whole lives are slowly offered back to the One from whom life came. The altar is no longer simply a place we visit. It becomes the shape of a life lived in grateful response to mercy.

Perhaps this offering happens in ways so ordinary that we scarcely notice them. We offer patience instead of resentment. We offer forgiveness when we would rather keep our wounds tightly closed. We offer our attention to a lonely friend, our strength to a weary neighbor, our quiet faithfulness in work that no one else sees. Every act of love becomes another small piece placed upon the altar—not because God delights in taking from us, but because He delights in filling what we willingly place into His hands.

There is a paradox hidden here. We often fear that surrender will diminish us, that opening our hands will leave us empty. Yet clenched fists cannot receive gifts. The heart that refuses to release control also struggles to receive grace. Again and again, the kingdom of God teaches us that life is found not by grasping, but by trusting. Like seeds buried beneath the soil, what is given in love is never destroyed; it is transformed.

Today, imagine your heart as an open hand before God. There may be plans you cannot control, burdens you have carried for too long, or hopes you are afraid to name. You do not need to force them onto the altar. Simply hold them before the One whose love is gentler than your fears. The God who received the offerings of Israel still receives every willing heart, and His refining fire is not the fire of destruction but of love—a love that burns away only what keeps us from becoming fully alive.

> *Today I open my hands and trust Love with all that I am.*
