## Day 26 — Reverence

**Leviticus 19:30**

**Psalm 27:4**

> *"Reverence is the quiet awakening of the heart to the holy presence that has always been near."*

God's instruction is beautifully simple: *"You shall keep my Sabbaths, and reverence my sanctuary."* Rest and reverence are held together, as though one naturally gives birth to the other. A hurried heart rarely notices what is holy. We become so occupied with accomplishing, producing, and moving from one task to the next that we pass by the quiet places where God is waiting to be found. Sabbath gently slows our pace, and reverence teaches our eyes to see.

The sanctuary was more than a sacred structure standing in the center of Israel's camp. It was a visible reminder that the Creator of heaven and earth desired to dwell among His people. Every time they lifted their eyes toward the tabernacle, they were invited to remember that they were not wandering alone. God's presence rested in their midst, quietly transforming an ordinary wilderness into holy ground. Reverence, then, was not fear of an inaccessible God, but loving attentiveness to the God who had drawn near.

David gives voice to this longing when he writes, *"One thing I have asked of Yahweh... that I may dwell in Yahweh's house all the days of my life, to see Yahweh's beauty."* His deepest desire is not for power, security, or success, but simply to behold the beauty of God. Beauty has a way of quieting the restless mind. We do not rush through a sunrise or hurry past a field covered in wildflowers if we truly wish to receive their gift. We linger. In much the same way, reverence is the willingness to remain present long enough for the beauty of God to reshape us.

Jesus revealed that this sanctuary was never meant to be confined to walls of stone or curtains of woven linen. He called Himself the true temple, and through Him the presence of God walked among ordinary people. He blessed meals at humble tables, prayed in lonely hillsides, welcomed children into His arms, and turned common moments into places of holy encounter. After His resurrection, the Spirit came to dwell within His people, making every faithful heart a living sanctuary. The holy place is no longer somewhere we travel to; it is the life we learn to inhabit with God.

This changes how we move through each day. Reverence is found not only in church pews or quiet chapels but in kitchens where meals are prepared with love, in offices where work is offered with integrity, in hospital rooms where compassion sits beside suffering, and beneath evening skies that quietly proclaim their Maker's glory. Every place can become a sanctuary when we remember that God is already there. The practice of reverence is simply learning to notice. We pause before speaking. We listen before reacting. We become attentive to the sacredness hidden within ordinary moments, trusting that every day contains invitations to behold the beauty of the Lord.

From Eden, where humanity walked with God in the cool of the day, to the tabernacle in the wilderness, from the temple in Jerusalem to Christ dwelling among us, and now through the Spirit within His people, the story of Scripture is the story of God's abiding presence. The journey has never been toward a more distant heaven but toward a deeper awareness that heaven has always been drawing near. One day the New Creation will reveal what reverence already begins to see: that the whole earth is filled with the glory of God, and every corner of creation is destined to become His dwelling place. Until then, we cultivate hearts that know how to linger in His presence, discovering that the deepest reverence is simply joyful attentiveness to the God who is with us.

> *Today I will slow my heart and behold the beauty of God's presence all around me.*
