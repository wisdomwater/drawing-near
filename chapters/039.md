## Day 39 — Care for the Poor

**Leviticus 25:35–38**

**Acts 2:44–47**

> *"Love is most clearly seen when another's burden quietly becomes our own."*

In the laws of Leviticus, God speaks with remarkable tenderness toward those who have fallen into poverty. The command is not merely to avoid harming them, but to uphold them, so that they may continue to live among the community with dignity and hope. Need was never to become an opportunity for personal gain. Instead, God's people were to remember that they themselves had once been strangers, oppressed and powerless, and that every blessing they now possessed had first been given by grace. The God who dwelt among them desired a community where no one was abandoned when life became difficult.

This reveals something essential about the heart of God. He does not see people primarily through the measure of their success or failure, but through the unchanging worth He has placed within them. Poverty, weakness, illness, grief, or hardship do not diminish a person's value. If anything, they become places where divine compassion draws especially near. Again and again throughout Scripture, God is revealed as One who bends toward those who are struggling—not with pity that looks down from a distance, but with love that comes alongside and shares the weight.

We all experience seasons of poverty, though not always in the same way. Some lack material resources. Others are poor in courage after disappointment, poor in hope after loss, poor in joy after long seasons of anxiety, or poor in strength after carrying burdens that seem too heavy. The invitation of this passage is wider than financial generosity alone. We are called to become people who notice where another's life has grown thin and quietly offer what God has first entrusted to us. Sometimes that gift is money. Often it is time, listening, encouragement, forgiveness, companionship, or simply the faithful willingness to remain present.

The first followers of Jesus embodied this beautiful vision. Luke tells us that they held their possessions with open hands and shared freely whenever anyone had need. Their generosity was not driven by obligation but by joy. Having discovered the immeasurable riches of God's love in Christ, they found it natural to let that love overflow into the lives of one another. Their shared meals, their glad hearts, and their willingness to bear one another's burdens became visible signs that God was indeed dwelling among His people. The Church itself became a living tabernacle of compassion.

This same Spirit continues to shape us today. We need not solve every problem or carry every burden to participate in God's mercy. We simply begin by asking, *Who has God placed before me today?* The lonely neighbor, the discouraged coworker, the grieving friend, the stranger who feels unseen—each becomes an opportunity to make room for God's kindness. As we learn to give without calculating what we might receive in return, our hearts slowly become freer. We discover that generosity is not the loss of something we own, but the joyful sharing of what has always been God's gift.

From the manna that fed Israel in the wilderness, to the laws that protected the poor, to the table fellowship of the early Church, and finally to the New Creation where there is no hunger, no exclusion, and no scarcity of love, Scripture tells one continuous story of divine generosity. God's deepest desire has always been to create a people who reflect His own openhearted compassion. As we uphold one another with quiet faithfulness, we become signs of His kingdom—a community where every person is welcomed, every burden is shared, and every table leaves room for one more.

> *Today I will receive God's generosity with gratitude and gladly become a source of hope for another.*
