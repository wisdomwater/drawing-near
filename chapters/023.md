## Day 23 — Welcome the Stranger

**Leviticus 19:33–34**

**Matthew 25:35**

> *"Every stranger is an opportunity to discover that God's welcome is wider than our fears."*

Among the many instructions in Leviticus, this one carries a remarkable tenderness. God does not simply tell Israel to tolerate the foreigner living among them. He tells them to love the stranger as they love themselves. They are to remember their own story, for they too once lived as strangers in a land not their own. Memory becomes the soil from which compassion grows. Those who remember being carried by mercy are less likely to withhold mercy from someone else.

This command reveals something profound about the heart of God. He is not only the God of those who already belong; He is the One who continually makes room for those who feel they do not. Throughout Scripture, God is gathering wanderers, exiles, refugees, and outsiders into His household. The tabernacle standing in the wilderness was itself a sign that God was journeying with a pilgrim people. His presence was never confined to places of comfort or certainty. Again and again, He met His people on the road, teaching them that home is ultimately found wherever He dwells among them.

Jesus carries this vision to its fullest expression. In His picture of the final judgment, He says, *"I was a stranger and you took me in."* It is one of the most astonishing statements He ever makes. He identifies Himself not with the powerful or admired, but with the overlooked and the vulnerable. To welcome the stranger is, in some mysterious way, to welcome Christ Himself. Every act of hospitality becomes more than kindness between people; it becomes an encounter with the hidden presence of God.

Hospitality begins long before someone crosses the threshold of our home. It begins in the posture of the heart. We all carry quiet boundaries that separate "us" from "them"—those who think differently, worship differently, come from another culture, or simply make us uncomfortable because they are unfamiliar. Fear naturally builds fences, but love patiently opens gates. The kingdom of God is formed wherever we become willing to see another person not first as a category or a problem, but as someone deeply loved by the Father.

There is another kind of stranger we sometimes overlook: the stranger within ourselves. There are places in our own hearts that remain unfamiliar—wounded places, fearful places, forgotten places we would rather keep hidden. Yet Christ approaches those places with the same gentle welcome He extends to every weary traveler. He does not enter by force. He knocks, waits, and invites. As we receive His compassion for the parts of ourselves we struggle to love, we often find our capacity to welcome others quietly expanding as well. The mercy we receive becomes the mercy we are able to give.

From the garden where humanity first walked with God, through Israel's wilderness journey, to Christ who made His dwelling among us, the story of Scripture is the story of divine hospitality. God continually welcomes those who have wandered far from home and prepares a table where strangers become family. The Church is called to be a living sign of that coming kingdom, a place where no one must earn a seat before they are seen with dignity and love. Every open door, every shared meal, every listening ear, and every act of welcome whispers the same enduring truth: God's house is larger than we imagined, and His heart has always been making room.

> *Today I will welcome others with the generous hospitality that God has first shown to me.*
