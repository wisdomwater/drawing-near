## Day 47 — Walking with God

**Leviticus 26:12**

**Genesis 3:8**

> *"The story of Scripture begins and ends with a God who longs to walk beside His people."*

Hidden within a single promise in Leviticus is the heartbeat of the entire book: *"I will walk among you, and will be your God, and you will be my people."* Every sacrifice, every festival, every priestly blessing, every act of cleansing has been leading toward this simple desire. God does not merely seek obedience from a distance. He desires communion. The tabernacle standing in the center of Israel's camp was never an end in itself. It was a sign that the Creator longs to dwell in the midst of ordinary human life, sharing the journey with His beloved people.

That promise reaches all the way back to the opening pages of Genesis. Before fear entered the human heart, God walked in the garden in the cool of the day. The image is quiet and deeply personal. We are not shown a distant ruler issuing commands from heaven, but a God who comes near, seeking relationship. The tragedy of Eden is not simply that humanity broke a commandment. It is that fear replaced trust. Instead of running toward the One who was drawing near, Adam and Eve hid among the trees, believing that love had somehow become a threat.

How often we continue that same pattern. When we become aware of our failures, our first instinct is often to hide. We withdraw behind busyness, self-reliance, distraction, or shame. We assume that God must be disappointed, waiting for us to repair ourselves before we dare step back into His presence. Yet the story of Scripture consistently reveals something different. God is always the One who comes looking. In the garden He called, "Where are you?" Not because He had lost His children, but because He longed for them to know they were still being sought.

Leviticus is God's answer to the hiding that began in Eden. The tabernacle becomes a new meeting place where heaven and earth begin to touch again. Through symbols of sacrifice, cleansing, Sabbath, and covenant, God patiently teaches His people that His deepest desire is not separation but reconciliation. The rituals point beyond themselves to a day when nothing would stand between God and humanity. In Christ that promise comes astonishingly close. He walks the dusty roads of Galilee, shares meals with ordinary people, welcomes the forgotten, and is called *Emmanuel*—"God with us." The God who walked in Eden now walks among us once more.

To walk with God is rarely dramatic. More often it is discovered in the quiet faithfulness of ordinary days. It is becoming aware that we are never truly alone as we prepare a meal, drive to work, sit beside a hospital bed, laugh with friends, or wait in silence before dawn. Brother Lawrence spoke of practicing the presence of God while washing dishes because he had learned that every place becomes holy when love is present. Walking with God is less about escaping daily life than awakening to the Companion who has always been walking beside us.

The Bible's great story forms a beautiful circle. It begins in a garden where God walks with humanity. It passes through the tabernacle and the temple, where His presence dwells among His people. It reaches its fullness in Christ, who walks among us in flesh and blood, and continues through the Church, where His Spirit makes His home within willing hearts. Finally, it ends in the New Creation, where God dwells openly with humanity and every barrier has fallen away. The deepest longing of God has never changed. He still comes looking in the cool of the day, inviting us to step out from our hiding places and discover that we have always been welcomed into His presence.

> *Today I will walk with the God who has always desired to walk with me.*
