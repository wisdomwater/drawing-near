## Day 48 — God Tabernacles Among Us

**Leviticus 26:11–12**

**John 1:14**

> *"The God who once pitched His tent among Israel has now made His home within the human story."*

Near the close of Leviticus, after all the sacrifices, festivals, priestly ministries, and covenant promises, God speaks the words toward which the entire book has been moving: *"I will set my tent among you... I will walk among you, and will be your God, and you will be my people."* This is the fulfillment of every symbol that has come before. The tabernacle was never simply a sacred structure standing in the wilderness. It was a declaration of God's heart. The Creator of heaven and earth desired to live among His people, sharing their journey through deserts and resting places alike.

The image of the tent is deeply beautiful. A tent is not a fortress built to keep others at a distance. It is a dwelling that can travel wherever the journey leads. God's presence was never confined to a mountain or hidden away behind walls. He chose to accompany His people through uncertainty, reminding them that wherever they wandered, they did not wander alone. The wilderness itself became holy because God was there. His home was placed at the center of the camp, a quiet witness that communion, not distance, has always been His deepest desire.

John opens his Gospel by returning to this very promise. *"The Word became flesh and lived among us."* The Greek word John chooses carries the beautiful sense of pitching a tent or tabernacling among humanity. In Jesus, the presence once symbolized by fabric, altars, and sacred spaces takes on a human face. The glory that filled the tabernacle now shines through a life full of grace and truth. God does not merely visit creation; He enters it completely. He eats at ordinary tables, walks dusty roads, weeps beside grieving friends, blesses children, and welcomes strangers. The tabernacle is no longer made of curtains but of flesh and blood.

This changes the way we understand God's nearness. We often imagine Him waiting for us in places set apart—church sanctuaries, quiet retreats, moments of extraordinary devotion. Those places are precious, yet Christ reveals that God is just as present in kitchens and workshops, on crowded streets and lonely paths, in hospital rooms and gardens, in laughter and in tears. The One who tabernacled among us has sanctified the ordinary. Every place becomes a meeting place when our hearts awaken to His presence.

There is another gentle wonder hidden in God's promise: *"My soul won't abhor you."* These words speak directly to one of humanity's oldest fears—the fear that if God truly knew us, He would turn away. Yet the whole movement of Scripture tells the opposite story. In Christ, God steps toward the very world marked by weakness, sorrow, and sin. He does not recoil from our humanity but embraces it. His presence is not fragile, threatened by our brokenness. It is healing, patient, and full of compassion. Love comes near precisely where it is most needed.

The story that begins in the garden with God walking among His children unfolds through the tabernacle in the wilderness, shines in the face of Christ, continues as the Spirit dwells within the Church, and reaches its glorious fulfillment in the New Creation, where *"the dwelling of God is with humanity."* Leviticus has been preparing us for this all along. Beneath every law and every symbol lies the unwavering desire of God to make His home with His beloved. As we welcome His presence into the ordinary moments of today, we discover that the true tabernacle has never merely been a place. It has always been a relationship of love in which God delights to dwell with His people.

> *Today I will welcome the God who joyfully makes His home among His people.*
