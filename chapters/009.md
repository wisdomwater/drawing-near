## Day 9 — Repairing What Is Broken

**Leviticus 6:1–7**

**Matthew 5:23–24**

> *"Love is not content with saying, 'I am sorry'; it longs to make whole what has been broken."*

Leviticus reminds us that our relationship with God cannot be separated from the way we treat one another. When someone had wronged a neighbor through dishonesty, oppression, or deceit, forgiveness was joined to restoration. What had been taken was to be returned, and even more than had been lost was to be given back. The offering at the altar was never meant to replace the work of repairing broken relationships. God's desire was not only to forgive hearts, but to heal communities.

There is a quiet wisdom here. Sin is never merely a private matter hidden away inside the soul. Like a stone dropped into a still pond, its ripples reach outward into the lives of others. Harsh words linger in memory. Broken trust leaves invisible scars. Neglected kindness creates empty spaces where love once might have flourished. God, who longs to dwell among His people, is concerned not only with individual hearts but with the bonds that hold His family together.

Jesus echoes this same vision in the Sermon on the Mount. He imagines a worshiper standing before the altar when a memory surfaces: a relationship remains fractured. His instruction is striking. Leave the gift. Seek reconciliation. Then return to worship. It is not because worship is unimportant, but because reconciliation is itself a form of worship. The God who receives our prayers also delights when estranged hearts begin the slow journey back toward one another.

Of course, not every relationship can be fully restored. Some wounds run deep. Some people are no longer with us. Others may not be ready—or able—to receive our attempts at peace. Scripture never asks us to control what belongs to another person's freedom. But it does invite us to keep our own hearts open. We can tell the truth where we have caused harm. We can make amends where they are possible. We can release bitterness into God's hands when reconciliation cannot yet be completed. Faithfulness is measured not by perfect outcomes but by a willingness to become agents of peace.

There is another beautiful detail in Leviticus: restoration included generosity. The one who had caused loss was asked to return not only what had been taken but something more besides. Grace has always moved beyond the strict mathematics of repayment. God's own love does not merely restore us to where we were; it continually gives more than we deserve. As we grow into His likeness, we begin to discover the quiet joy of becoming people who leave others richer in hope, kindness, and dignity than we found them.

Today, ask gently whether there is anything in your life that longs for repair. Perhaps it is a conversation that has been postponed, a letter that needs to be written, forgiveness that quietly waits to be offered, or simply a prayer for someone from whom you have grown distant. Do not carry this as a burden of guilt, but as an invitation of love. The God who is making all things new delights in every small act that mends what has been torn. Each step toward reconciliation becomes another way His dwelling place is built among us.

> *Today I welcome the grace that heals hearts and restores what love can mend.*
